The Good Life

In Science, Technology, Society

The concept of "the good life" within Science, Technology, and Society (STS) is a dynamic and evolving notion, transcending simplistic definitions of material wealth or technological advancement alone. It draws inspiration from ancient philosophical traditions, such as Aristotle's eudaimonia, which emphasizes human flourishing, virtue, and the fulfillment of one's potential (Scribd, n.d.). However, in an STS context, the good life is critically examined through the lens of how scientific and technological developments shape, enable, or even hinder its attainment. This involves questioning not just what technologies can do for us, but also what kind of lives they help us create and whether those lives align with broader ethical and societal values (Quizgecko, n.d.). STS perspectives often highlight that technology's contribution to the good life is far from straightforward. While innovations in medicine, communication, and sustainable energy undoubtedly offer pathways to improved health, greater connectivity, and environmental stewardship, they also present complex ethical dilemmas and social challenges. For instance, the pursuit of longevity through biotechnology raises questions about equality of access and the societal implications of an aging population. Similarly, pervasive digital technologies, while facilitating communication, can also contribute to social isolation, privacy concerns, and the spread of misinformation, challenging our notions of a well-lived life (Prezi, n.d.; SlideShare, n.d.). Crucially, STS emphasizes that "the good life" is not a universal, fixed blueprint but is often shaped by cultural contexts, societal priorities, and power dynamics. What constitutes a good life in one society or for one individual may differ significantly from another. Therefore, STS encourages a participatory and democratic approach to technological development, where diverse voices and values are considered in shaping future innovations. This involves moving beyond a purely technocentric view of progress towards one that integrates social, ethical, and environmental considerations from the outset, ensuring that technology serves human well-being in a holistic and equitable manner (CODE Berlin, n.d.). Ultimately, the STS inquiry into the good life is a continuous reflection on how we can harness scientific knowledge and technological capabilities to create a future where human flourishing is genuinely fostered for all. It demands critical thinking about the impacts of our technological choices, a commitment to ethical responsibility in innovation, and a willingness to redefine progress in terms of genuine human well-being and a sustainable relationship with our planet. By engaging with these complex questions, STS aims to guide us toward a more thoughtful and deliberate construction of the good life in an increasingly techno-scientific world (ResearchGate, n.d.).
